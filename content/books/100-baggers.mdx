### The one principle

The most important idea, worth repeating: you need a business with a high return on capital **and** the ability to reinvest at that same high return for years and years. Growth plus a rising multiple, working together, is what produces the truly mammoth result.

### SQGLP

The essence of the 100-baggers, distilled to five elements:

- **S** — Size is small
- **Q** — Quality is high, for both the business and its management
- **G** — Growth in earnings is high
- **L** — Longevity in both quality and growth
- **P** — Price is favorable

### The coffee-can portfolio

Find the best stocks you can and let them sit for ten years. Almost no cost, easy to manage — but the real benefit is subtler: it keeps your worst instincts from hurting you. It forces a longer time horizon and stops the frantic checking, buying, and selling. You don't put anything in the can you don't believe is a good ten-year bet.

### Moats

A 100-bagger needs a high return on capital for a long time, and a moat is what defends it: a strong brand, high switching costs, network effects, being the lowest-cost producer, or sheer scale. As one investor put it — in a market where you need 55% just to break even, only one competitor survives, and the firm that gets there can keep prices just high enough to keep everyone else out.

### Essentials

1. **Growth** — value-added growth in sales and earnings per share, not growth diluted away by new shares.
2. **Lower multiples preferred** — the higher the price you pay, the higher the growth rate has to be to rescue it. A PEG near 1 is a rough sanity check.
3. **Economic moats are a necessity** — they're what allow the long runway of high returns.
4. **Smaller companies preferred** — generally below a ~$1B market cap; a more fruitful place to hunt.
