### Valuation in one idea

A P/E ratio is how many years' worth of earnings you're paying for. At a 20 P/E you're paying $20 for every $1 of earnings — roughly 20 years to get your money back if earnings don't grow. The higher the multiple, the more you're paying and the longer the payback, which is why a higher P/E is called "more expensive."

### Free cash flow

Free cash flow strips the non-cash noise out of net income to show the real cash a business produces:

> Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures = Free Cash Flow

That's the cash actually available to return to shareholders.

### Yields and valuations

Bond yields and stock valuations move inversely — when yields rise, valuations tend to fall, and vice versa. When stock yields fall far below bond yields, stocks may be getting expensive and due for a correction.

### Patience

Even Buffett underperformed. From 1996–99, at the peak of the tech bubble, Berkshire returned 74% against the S&P's 155%, and people said value investing was dead. He refused to copy the speculation — and from 2000–03, as the bubble collapsed, Berkshire returned 29% while the index lost ~45%.
